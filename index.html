In Search of the Perfect Cup of Coffee


Not everyone likes coffee, but then not all cups of coffee are equal.

A slug of instant caffeine shares very little in common with the best that coffee has to offer.
The good news is that the explosion in the number of artisanal coffee roasters and coffee shops now trading on UK high-streets means that most of us can easily experience and afford a coffee epiphany.

But once you know how coffee should taste, how do you go about elevating your daily cuppa?

At home, you might obsess about how to get the best from your chosen method of brewing. But you will never get a decent cup unless you give serious thought to what happened to the coffee before you spooned it into your coffee maker.

You want a special brew? Start thinking about:
  • How the beans were processed before they got to the roaster.
  • How the beans have been roasted (lighter roasts allow more complex flavours to come through).
  • How long it was since the beans were roasted (grinding the beans too early after roasting makes for a thinner less satifying flavour).
  • Think about grinding the beans just before you brew your coffee. It really makes a difference.
  • Experiment with what brewing method gives you the strength of extract that you want (pour-over or stove-top mokka; aeropress or coffee machine).

Each element in the process has its own rabbit hole that you can get lost in.

My advice would be to:
  1. Find a local roaster that you trust; someone with whom you can discuss the sort of coffee you enjoy (everyone has their own palate).
  2. Get yourself a good hand grinder (they are a lot cheaper that electric ones).
  3. Keep a week's supply of beans in a container that limits exposure to air and direct sunlight (store the rest in ziplock bags in the freezer).
  4. Use a stove-top mokka-pot if you like a strong cup of coffee (not the same as over-roasted).
  5. Try oat milk rather than cow (it gives a smoother texture).
